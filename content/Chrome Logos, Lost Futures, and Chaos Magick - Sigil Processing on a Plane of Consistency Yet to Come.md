# Chrome Logos, Lost Futures, and Chaos Magick; Sigil Processing on a Plane of Consistency Yet to Come

This is a call for the development of spiritual practices on the heterodox left. To elaborate: it is a call for spiritual practices grounded in technics and aesthetics; it is a call for an anti-representational or non-representational political engagement. It bends towards a spiritual technical practice that is not about meaning making in a chaotic world, nor prophesy, nor predictive modeling. This is a call for intentional mark-making practices that radiate "becoming" outwards. Crystalization.

![[snowflake.gif]]

It is about a reckoning with our losses and defeats on the left, about checking in with the darker and lighter souls of our collective project. It is about admitting what the right is doing so well, while resisting the lure of a dialectic hall of mirrors. There is no gambit which will beat them at the game they are playing, they have constructed irony-clad metagames, and of course it is metagames all the way down.

The frightening strength of the various political movements associated with the new right comes in their 's ability to cohere incoherent subjectivities, paradigms, and ethos. Liberal and leftist commentators foolishly await the moment when this will break down, when the movement's contradictions will finally alienate the right voter base, that accelerationist fascism will finally be forced slow down and diversify.

Wise up: these funhouse mirrors do not shatter, or rather, when they shatter, they generate _N_ number of new perspectives that begin a process of coherence again.

> Principle of asignifying rupture: A rhizome may be broken, shattered at a given spot, but it will start up again on one of its old lines, or on new lines. You can never get rid of ants because they form an animal rhizome that can rebound time and again after most of it has been destroyed. (Deleuze and Guattari, A Thousand Plateaus, 9)

What is the source of this science-fictional strength? I think we need recourse to think the power of symbols and signals which do not collapse back into a sober, one-to-one representational schema ("x is a dog whistle for y"). We need recourse to an affective semiotics that acknowledges that the power of fiction is never in the triumph of truth over artifice. Sobriety is an anesthetic. Attunement.

To begin, the 3D-rendered, shiny chrome corporate logo of the 1980s are an excellent if not strange place to start. This will lead us into conversations about reality as it relates to the future. The polemics and questions of the last 30 years ("where has the future gone?", "who owns the future?", "the future is cancelled") are all relevant here. The question is about becoming, about individuation, about transductive reformation.

## Cyber Logos, Cosmic Pathos, Communist Ethos

A friend, media performance artist Odette Stout, recently shared an odd article with me they teach in their motion graphics class called [[The Chrome Age]] : The Dawn of Virtual Reality which students found extremely resonant. Why? Stout explains:

1. The author has a rough-an-tumble attitude ("giving pugnacious", "Lilith Faire logs on") that gives undergraduate students ways into critically discerning the author's position and subsequently articulating their own position relative to the author's polemic.
2. The text is grounded in an intuitive if not familiar tension to most young people: that the digital lays some contested claim to the primacy of reality over the rest of lived experienced, and there is a relevant disciplinary techno-socio-economics around this.
3. The article allows us to situate emergent cybersigil tattoo, fashion, and graphic design aesthetics as part of a nonlinear techno-aesthetic genealogy.

It this the third point, the arc between chrome logos of the 1980s and today's aesthetics, not present in the essay but elaborated by Odette pedagogically, which caught my eye. Because, as articulated in the second point, there is a widespread, almost implicit, sense amongst the digital media arts community that aesthetic sensation and creation allows us to retrieve, reclaim, and re-articulate the claims laid on reality by the digital. Master's tools, seize the means of production, and all that. It proceeds however to lay claim on the digital, which then is often conflated with "the scientific", by means of aesthetic experience, and aesthetics alone.

The article was published in _Leonardo_, a journal dedicated to investigating the nascent "art-science nexus". _Leonardo_ was founded in 1968 by rocket scientist turned kinetic artist Frank Malina. The journal and its broad community has a complex, important, and influential history. That [Frank Malina was a member of the communist party who was investigated by the FBI](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/air-space-magazine/who-was-frank-malina-180973146/) while he worked as a head researcher in defense contracting is a detail that would be confusing to someone looking at the Leonardo organization today, which seems to foster discourse that mirrors the de-politicized, institutionalized, neoliberal rhetoric about the arts inspiring technological innovation, and so on. But the story maps onto narratives that would be familiar to students of cold war technoscience and its intersection with arts.

I feel strongly that thinking the arc of the 1970s and 80s to today is one of the best exercises we can do to understand our contemporary situation. Having observed the 50-year anniversary of[ 1968](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1191/030913201678580467), I feel also there is a certain something about half-century rhythmic resonance; offbeat). The same year Shannon published this essay, 1995, English media theorist Richard Barbrook and Andy Cameron published the essay "[The California Ideology](https://monoskop.org/images/d/dc/Barbrook_Richard_Cameron_Andy_1996_The_Californian_Ideology.pdf)". The essay tells the mid-century failings of technoliberatory ethos. In three acts:

1. Vietnam-era counterculture.
2. counterculture bifurcates into "New Left" politics and reactionary anti-political "New Age" communalists and technologists.
3. The "New Left" fails to counter Reagan's ascension from governor of California to president, ushering in global doctrines of neoliberal economics, while "New Age" communalists are transformed into Silicon Valley cyberlibertarian entrepreneurs.

(Adam Curtis documentary films "All Watched Over by Machines of Loving Grace" is very good on this, if not a bit bombastic). Of course, we are not only haunted by the spectre of the California Ideology, its undead world is consuming us whole.

One of the questions I am trying to ask in this writing is, broadly speaking, what relics can be salvaged from the monasteries of techno-optimism? What, in the diffuse and non-linear genealogy of a techno-liberatory ethos, are germs of solidarity waiting to crystalize? And, what chemical reagents do they require to do so? The arc between mid-1980s chrome 3D rendered logos and mid-2020s cybersigil provides a unique context in which to ask this question.
I have written [a bit](https://keep.lib.asu.edu/items/171591) about the critique of art-science hybrids, in particular their institutionally enforced fealty to apolitical and anti-subjectivist ideologies. Chrome logos and and cybersigil allow us to see, perhaps, that this is not all that is missing from the equation.

## Chrome Logos and Art-Science Utopia

To turn directly to the Chrome Logo, we revisit Shannon, who constructs an alternative genealogy of computer graphics and 3D environments ("virtual reality") originating from the [[hyper-virtual]] and perhaps [[hyper-real]] digital 3D objects of corporate "chrome" logos. Virtual Reality for Shannon is a synthesis generated by an intermingling between artistic and scientific worldviews and workflows. Art gives up an emphasis on the singularity of the art object for digital objects composed of code (""the infinite perfect copies of digital art are closer in character to the scientist's reiterative verification process than the artist's singular expressive event.") On the other hand, science gives up its caricatured anchoring of truth in objectivity in favor of subjectivist expression and mark-making.

Are we really after art-science hybrids? What promises are left for art-science to give? Shannon writes that, looking back on the emergence of VR, "in retrospect, were actually witnessing the alchemical moment of science fusing with art and popular entertainment." There is certainly an alchemy at work here, but in my view this is an impoverished account of science and art.

One richer understanding of science and art comes from French philosopher and cybernetician Gilbert Simondon's [Genesis of Technicity](https://www.e-flux.com/journal/82/133160/the-genesis-of-technicity/). In his argument (depicted in a diagram by Simon O'Sullivan below), scientific knowledge is the antecedent development from stemming from philosophy and technicity. However, philosophy and technology are only earlier bifurcations from an original magic unity between the human and world, which he describes in his essay:

>  technicity results from a phase shift of a unique, central, and original mode of being in the world: the magical mode; the phase that balances out technicity is the religious mode of being. **Aesthetic thought appears at the neutral point, between technics and religion, at the moment of the splitting of the primitive magical unity:** it is not a phase, but rather a permanent reminder of the rupture of unity of the magical mode of being, as well as a reminder of the search for its future unity.

If Aesthetics is an imperfect compromise between the primordial schism of the magical universe, it may be helpful to see artist's desire for an art-science hybrid as a (misled) way of resolving that schism, of "searching for its future unity" as Simondon says.

![[Pasted image 20250311200729.png]]

What is then this magical unity? Simondon:

> The magical universe is structured according to the most primitive and meaningful of organizations: that of the reticulation of the world into privileged places and privileged moments. A privileged place, a place that has a power, is one that drains from within itself all the force and efficacy of the domain it delimits; it summarizes and contains the force of a compact mass of reality; it summarizes and governs it, as a highland governs and dominates a lowland; the elevated peak is the lord of the mountain, just as the most impenetrable part of the woods is where all its reality resides. **The magical world is thus made of a network of places and of things that have a power and that are bound to other things and other places that also have a power.**

![](https://singularimages.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/12-29-20-ki6a9793-40x60-1.jpg)
(reticulation pattern)

I am however excited by the rhetorical power the author attributes to the 3D chrome logo: "chrome logos convinced the public that artificial worlds could eventually become 'real'" and that the logos were a spearhead for "hi-tech entertainment industry." Chrome 3D style's charm is in its fiction. Shannon describes the 3D re-constructions of reality as missing crucial aspects of reality such as light:

> The original secret behind the chrome look was that the highlight or shiny spot on an object could be colored independently from the rest of the object, though both received illumination from the same algorithm. Programmers, not in the habit of mixing colors, naively used primary colors: red, green, blue, cyan, magenta, yellow, black, and white. Light is rarely if ever pure white, however-a fact most artists and color scientists know well. Programmers nevertheless conceived of color intellectually rather than perceptually, and lights were invariably set at loo% white.

"The Chrome Age" is characterized by these contingent mistakes and missteps. Its bravado is that knows the power of artifice.  
![[Pasted image 20250311200609.png]]
In the thirty years since Sheridan wrote this paper, we have seen the many powers of the chrome logos. For one, these days, we see the power of artifice made impotent by the mocking post-irony of nostalgic design.

![](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ffiverr-res.cloudinary.com%2Fimages%2Ft_main1%2Cq_auto%2Cf_auto%2Cq_auto%2Cf_auto%2Fgigs%2F351125635%2Foriginal%2F7b7f3d10e87484ad8bbcf267e4d192005451df3b%2Fmake-vhs-80s-retro-neon-style-3d-chrome-synthwave-logo-intro-video.jpg&f=1&nofb=1&ipt=fe85f8123323a27df8dc3c08f2ad777cb5438adf5986ae2aed97abe08f7204e8&ipo=images)

In a time when the internet's dead beaches are awash with backwards-looking synthwave chrome logos, we must ask serious questions about the affective power of aesthetics and mark-making.
The chrome logo and its undead antecedents may have more to tell us about how we got into our political situation and the currents, viscosity, and ecology of the waters we must wade in the next decades. Intuition alone will not save us. Arms-crossed resentment and finger wagging will not save us. We know, we know. We feel a need for transformation, but we don't know yet what we will become. On the backend, these symbols are generating of sorts of subjectivities. We have to avoid turning this into a moralizing affair. We need an intellectual intuition (see Whitehead, Concept of Nature) from which we may rally forth an intentionality towards becoming born of desire, courage, and spirit. Shadow work, portal work.

This requires us to double down on Shannon's championing of chrome logos, which is to say that the 1980s chrome logo was not just designer trash, but a potent sigil for becoming. It was a powerful thing which moves towards a participate in a magical world. While the chrome logo has been captured (co-opted) by reactionary aesthetics, its intentional charge may find reanimation not in denigrating and post-ironic nostalgia but in generative intentional and intuitive practices courageously attuned to and attenuated by affective feeling.

## Lost Futures: Where Did the Future Go?

Indeed, the Shannon text emphasizes the rhetorical power of the chrome logo: "chrome logos convinced the public that artificial worlds could eventually become 'real'" and that the logos were a spearhead for "hi-tech entertainment industry." More than generating a passing aesthetic experience, the logo is ascribed a world-making power.

Digital and in particular 3D image making then marks a unique historical moment where artists and scientists swap perspectives called "the Chrome Age": "the infinite perfect copies of digital art are closer in character to the scientist's reiterative verification process than the artist's singular expressive event." The figure of the designer looms large; the designer is an envoy of market demand and industry subjectivity, portrayed as stealing from the more innocent if not unorthodox interplay between art and science, The "Chrome Age" is punctuated by a bait and switch in which industry fails to cash the checks that science has written: "Not only do they continue to serve as ideal, sterilized packaging for the evening news, but chrome logos give us a traditional image of riches to replace what we have lost as our financial exchanges are effected increasingly with plastic and paper." This is interesting as a pre-"dot-com" boom perspective suggesting a pre-y2k "gilded" moment. In our own time, our technology giants are dealt none of the financial blows described by the author about graphic firms. For us, schadenfreude is extremely rare.

Throughout the essay, I am reminded of two strands of thought that would come later, one of them is cultural theorist Mark Fisher and the CCRU's conceptual making, writing, and thinking practices related to "lost futures" and "hauntology". Hauntological survey and production selects aesthetic territories that are resonate with the virtualities of "lost futures"; worlds generated by science fiction, technoscientific optimism, and 1980s and 90s neoliberal and globalizing capitalists.

At the same time, the situation is more complex: Fisher, also a music critic, wrote that jungle music "sounded like the future rushing in", a remark which resonates with his collaborator [[Kodwo Eshun]]'s writing about musical practices as "future rhythm machines". Liquid metal. The music of Burial, for instance, unfurls hauntologically in that its train hall reverb seems to echo with the libidinal liberation of jungle music, though no such machine ever arrived at the station. On the other side of the helix, vaporwave re-generates the chrome logos, now weirded by their outing of place and time.

There is an earlier aesthetic resonance. [[Raygun gothic]]: chromed out sci-fi space diner. Maybe it's a western too; cue Daniel Lanois verbed out steel guitar on Eno's Apollo: Atmospheres and Soundtracks. Cyberpunk. Mirrorshades. Read Bruce Sterling's intro to that infamous volume:

> Mirrored sunglasses have been a Movement totem since the early days of '82. The reasons for this are not hard to grasp. By hiding the eyes, mirrorshades prevent the forces of normalcy from realizing that one is crazed and possibly dangerous. They are the symbol of the sunstaring visionary, the biker, the rocker, the policeman, and similar outlaws. Mirrorshades—preferably in chrome and matte black, the Movement's totem colors—appeared in story after story, as a kind of literary badge.

Burial (who now even features in a Spotify playlist called "hauntology") and Vaporwave, to varying degrees, deal in an optimism born of grief for a future lost. (Lost to what, to whom? by what means? Historical contingency? Will to power?) In any case, something is lost: "A telltale sign of a new era is a shift in social strata whereby a despised vocation suddenly assumes high status while a respected profession sinks into oblivion" (Shannon again). But it is not quite grief; it is any thing but resolved, it is a lingering in the abyss of loss that lures a nihilism.

Nietzsche writes that the warring against belief inevitably ends in nihilism. [Recently commentators](https://pod.link/1512615438/episode/a1a5e363f293f3ab60b72da3b47074c1) have begun to admit hauntology's fickle politics; that MAGA is a particularly well operationalized hauntology. There are larger questions here about what might make MAGA or men's rights movements so well operationalized. Walter Benjamin characterized fascism through an increasingly aestheticization of politics. The rebel without a cause always harbors a secret cause, even if it is unknown to them.

The second resonance is with [an iconic debate](https://davidgraeber.org/videos/david-graeber-vs-peter-thiel-where-did-the-future-go-2/) between the anarchist anthropologist David Graeber and venture capitalist Peter Thiel from 2014 organized around the question "Where Did the Future Go?" From my perspective, I observe a remarkably salient anticipation of our own political-aesthetic moment. Graeber did his thing, his opening remarks recapitulate the thesis of his 2012 hit article "[Of Flying Cars and the Declining Rate of Profit](https://thebaffler.com/salvos/of-flying-cars-and-the-declining-rate-of-profit)," which elaborates a history of today's so-called boring dystopia: capital hoards wealth by pedaling liberatory techno-fantasies (flying cars, shorter working hours, miracle drugs) to working class people. A key part of Graeber's argument is built around the culture and economics of scientific research, in particular as it regards bureaucracy:

> Research and development is still driven by giant bureaucratic projects. What has changed is the bureaucratic culture. The increasing interpenetration of government, university, and private firms has led everyone to adopt the language, sensibilities, and organizational forms that originated in the corporate world. Although this might have helped in creating marketable products, since that is what corporate bureaucracies are designed to do, in terms of fostering original research, the results have been catastrophic.

Thiel sets in, playing a different game entirely. Thiel _agrees_ with Graeber: we were not delivered what we were promised and we need to cut the red tape. The replacement motor for a system of checks and balances and forms is not the distributed consensus-making celebrated by anarchists, but techno-monarchs who are ability to dictate the world from the top down. Even free market economics, which neoliberal economists regarded as a kind "distributed computer" that optimized economic exchange through abstract machines of supply and demand, is not efficient enough for the kinds of complexity that Thiel (and his ilk) want to sort for. For Thiel, market competition is red tape and corporate monopoly of production, research, and power is the future.

Where did the future go? Here it is again.

![[Pasted image 20250303081354.png]]

Besides the finer points of Thiel and Graeber's discussion, there is an incredible thing happening at the intertwined registers of affect, performance, and rhetoric. We watch as Graeber seems to clock Thiel's performative strategy in real time. Thiel shows up in an outfit mimicking Graeber: blazer, white shirt with top button unbuttoned. Thiel remarks in passing on their visual mirroring, as if to subtly foreshadow the rhetorical doubling he was about to employ.

In light of Thiel's moves, Graeber's endearing vocal charisma, stringing together ideas punctuated by his endearing verbal pauses, seems flat or quaint. Heartbreaking. They agree on many things: poignantly, the university's over professionalized grant-driven metric bureaucracy has made the cultural figure of the idiosyncratic professor out of date -- referring no doubt to Graeber himself. Again and again, Graeber fails to let the grit and grime of down and dirty anarchism do the rhetorical work that Thiel's PayPal slick, frictionless sheen seems to do with ease. Thiel's solution is scale. Not competition, but monopoly. Totalitarian, of course, but in a way that lures the treacherous fantasy of techno-optimism.

![](https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-Nqvitq7tkcU/Xq9oYytWzPI/AAAAAAAAhzk/tIeGtAmC7pUM-x_bR2RpZXkrcVzBUkKbACLcBGAsYHQ/s1600/Annihilation%2B018.jpg)

It would not be difficult to imagine that Thiel's performance inspired the aliens in Jeff Vandermeer's Annihilation, Naomi Klein's Doppelgaenger, or James Cameron's liquid metal Terminator mode, absorbing and reflecting. The nimbleness of anarchism, its countercultural clout, absorbed. These days, we live in a [PayPal Mafia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PayPal_Mafia) state, of course. JD Vance, Elon Musk, and Mark Zuckerberg all got their start with Thiel money, not to mention philosopher of the D@rk Enlightenment Curtis Y@rvin.

I am not suggesting that Graeber's anarchistic critique of neoliberal capitalism shelters a map that secretly leads us to technofascism. If we are to center Graeber here at all, it is because he was a beloved luminary of emancipatory politics who very likely foresaw how neoliberal capitalism would pave the way for the new right, but could not defeat it. Graeber's article was a hit because it resonated with people, perhaps people he never would have anticipated.

The question of grief may shed light here; that some are able to bypass grieving entirely, to put their blinders on and set their path to the heart of the sun. Most of us though, need to process, but also need ways of carrying loss with us. Graeber died in 2020. Though his legacy is so much more than flying cars, he made it part of his brand. Resentment bait, whatever the intension, lures a politics of self-annihilation.

## Sigil Processing

Returning to the chrome logo. Typically we understand a logo as a symbol. Danish autonomist marxist brand theorist Adam Arvidsson ("Brands", 2005) writes that "brands build on the immaterial labour of consumers: their ability to create an ethical surplus (a social bond, a shared experience, a common identity) through productive communication." In other words, The word "McDonalds" does not only communicate the identity of a corporation, it lures the generation of excess feeling, associative, rhizomatic, affirming (even if the affirmative is of a negative feeling like disgust). This notion of a surplus upsets the tight interlock of the signifier->signified. In other words, the power of a brand is that we don't know ahead of time what it could mean. Corporations of course do not care what "they mean", they are interested in what branding "can do".

Arvidsson considers the origins of the word brand; a red-hot piece of metal which painfully sears skin and flesh, transforming the body at the level of the nervous system. The relation of the word logo with _logos_ suggests a little concrete machine whose power is the proliferation of abstract machines. A logic of sensation rather than of sense-making. In both cases, it is clear that these symbols are powerful operators at a pre-conscious or pre-subjective level (at the level of affect of feeling). The spilling out of surplus labor comes not from a cognitive, rational reception of a message ("McDonalds is an affordable place to eat") but, to be coarse, its rhetorical power. Logos build worlds through their capacious generation of modes of thinking-feeling.

Comic book artist and producer Grant Morrison has described corporate logos as sigils (as opposed to their more banal and commonplace understanding as symbols). This is exciting because symbolic interpretation often ignores emergent sensation that lead to un-prestatable (unpredictable) experiences and transformations. For instance: "X stands for Y", or "X represents Y". In other words the symbol stands in for something else promised ahead of time by the stability of a transcendental meaning-making system, bypassing the sensorial experience entirely. "Red means passion" only approaches empirical truth because of cumulative red-passional sensations of the body, over the long arc of culture.

In contrast to the symbol, the sigil means nothing at all. How can we say this when we are aware of the deep intentionality of the design of the logo, for instance? Let's talk about Austin Osman Spare, a British magician, artist, and occultist, and his interest in _sigilization_ (note, a verb).

![](https://images.squarespace-cdn.com/content/v1/60e8deffd230b9076e95fe07/fee03688-f4db-4b44-ab56-f6919481d101/Frame14.png)

First, let's reiterate how a symbol operates. Matt Lee writes:

> There are a number of models that can be distinguished: a symbol can _stand for_ something; it can _point to_ something; it can _contain_ something or it can _communicate_ something.  In each situation the symbol is essentially an adjunct of ‘something’, which we might describe as its meaning. 

He goes on to describe sigils:

> > It is important to distinguish very clearly Spare’s form of sigilisation from any traditional notion of symbolism. [...] Spare uses symbols in a way that is far closer to an artistic use of stroke or colour.  The paint stroke does not stand for, contain, transmit or somehow make us able to see the real picture behind the strokes and colours but is in fact the picture itself.  This process is a manifestation or creation.  It is an act itself, not a means.  This active symbolisation disturbs the very concept of symbols themselves, with a split in Spare’s work between  ‘symbols’ and ‘sigils’.  In The Book of Pleasure Spare focuses explicitly on the construction of both symbols and sigils.  It is in one of the sections on sigils, subtitled ‘the psychology of believing’, that we find the following: “_We are not the object by the perception, but by becoming it_”[[i]](#\_edn1).

Lee's linking of Spare's sigilization to Deleuzoguattarian becoming is a fertile ground we will return to. But let's consider an example first.

![[Screenshot 2025-03-04 132830.png]]

Consider: Make America Great Again. Leftists and liberal wring their hands: "what does MAGA mean anyway!" It means nothing at all, of course. The red MAGA hat is a sigil. Its red is a pure, live, exciting aesthetic experience; it is the red of flushed rosy skin and engorged genitalia. Branded red like a hot red brand, seeking pleasure in pain.

A thousand lines of flight explode out of a red cap, generating dark crystal consistency across a new plan. The continuous defeat of the left lies in the right's incredible power to cohere desires on a different plane of consistency we have not seen before. In other words: Anti-vax yoga moms, Three percenters, and gamer boys all create a resonant assemblage that **literally** vibes together.

In Deleuzoguattarian processual semiotics, the sigil creates fertility for an asignifying particle. Consider the Pink Panther:

> The Pink Panther imitates nothing, it reproduces nothing, it paints the world its color, pink on
> pink; this is its becoming-world, carried out in such a way that it becomes imperceptible itself, asignifying, makes its rupture, its own line of flight, follows its "aparallel evolution" through to the end. (ATP, 11).

![](https://m.media-amazon.com/images/M/MV5BZjQyNTk1M2EtNDAzYi00MTA3LTk0NWUtNzU0NzJkZWE3OTYzXkEyXkFqcGc@._V1_.jpg)

If a sigil signifies nothing, what does it do and how does it work? Now we can speak a bit about Deleuze and Guattari's conceptual and experimental practice of becoming. First, [[Becoming]] is not a metaphor, it is not symbolic, it is metaphysically, affectively, psychically, and virtually **real**: "Above all, becoming does not occur in the imagination, even when the imagination reaches the highest cosmic or dynamic level, [...] Becomings-animal are neither dreams nor phantasies. They are perfectly real."

![[Pasted image 20250311210511.png]]

One enters into a becoming-animal, a becoming-woman, a becoming-child, but it is never a journey from A to B. "A becoming is not a correspondence between relations. But neither is it a resemblance, an imitation, or, at the limit, an identification." It does not proceed via resemblance, but rather a de-coding across difference. Consider the becoming animal:

> "For if becoming animal does not consist in playing animal or imitating an animal, it is clear that the human being does not "really" become an animal any more than the animal "really" becomes something else. Becoming produces nothing other than itself. We fall into a false alternative if we say that you either imitate or you are. What is real is the becoming itself, the block of becoming, not the supposedly fixed terms through which that which becomes passes." (ATP)

You participate in a becoming-imperceptible, but you never know ahead of time when it will sweep you up and where it will take you. Becoming thus relies on radical experimentation marked by intention but without destination. Sigilization generates a block of becoming. Intentionality emerges as pre-personal creative force in the forging of a sigil; you start to sing but you are not becoming-singer; instead you are interceded by a becoming-bird, a becoming-angel, or a becoming-whale. It's not about you, man.

Lee:

> Sigilisation marries a true experimentalism of _automatic drawing_ with the conscious thought that ‘this X is our desire’, a desire no longer possessed by us but by which we are possessed.  Spare is Deleuzian in the sense that he no longer wants a focus on the ‘magician’ as controller, perceiver or creator.  Sparean sorcery is a technique not of ego dissolution but of practical experimentation with a pre-individual plane of immanence, the ocean of becoming.

## Chrome Logos and Cybersigils

What then of our chrome logos? The futurity promised by virtual reality is less relevant today than the sigils that preceded it. The cyber-sigil is a concatenation of cybernetics (from the Greek _kubernetes_ meaning to steer) and sigil, the aforementioned magical mark-making practice. Clickbait news stories remark that cybersigil is a tattooing and design style for a digital age. Of course, it looks very cool -- a steeled, quasi-black metal insignia. There does seem that magical practices are coming back, and so it stands to reason that cybersigil resonates as well with magic.

But it is just as easy to reason that many young people who are drawn to magical practices do as an aesthetic gesture. And we know from the last 50 years not only that aesthetics are easily co-opted, but that many are there only to absorb cultural cache. What a pity! Even among those who take it seriously, there will many who consider practicing magic within the sphere of their own life, their own interpersonal sphere or career, or those who think that politics is too mundane a field to be attenuated by magic.

Consider philosopher Isabelle Stengers:

> The witches learned this art in the moment of greatest distress, during the election of Ronald Reagan in 1980. It is then that the mutation of a tradition, the Wicca tradition (re-)born in England and exported to California, was produced. It then became a field for experimentation, cultivating the art of rituals able to give the inheritors of feminist, nonviolent, anti-imperialist, ecological struggles the strength to resist the ordeal. The recipes of the witches were thus reinvented in a political mode, whilst conserving their capacity to appeal explicitly to what does not stem from goodwill but from the event that makes people capable of thinking and feeling. - Capitalist Sorcery

Stengers goes on to say that not everyone will need to call themselves witches. I add: they may be artists, designers, activists, community organizers, writers. But above all we must be experimentalists who develop ways to preserve "the strength to resist."

A sigil is unrecognizable at first, it is a breaking of pattern. A sigil is emblematic, it is emblematic of nothing at all, but it is real, its charge is real. A sigil is a signal without a message, but it is concresced through a creative, involutional, and intentional becoming. A sigil is capacious, it is a noise: "what was that?" It is pure noise, the intention of the sigil (the content of the signal) is entirely unreproducable when it reaches its receiver; it has been transformed by its own transmission: sigilization.

We are reminded that by no means are either Simondon's primordial magical unity or its future reunification utopias devoid of strife and struggle. To move towards a magical reunification, towards a yet-unknown plane of consistency on which the left can resonate, requires the experimentation with what we now call technics, aesthetics, and politics, but will be so entirely transformed by the process of sigilization that they will be unrecognizable to us.
